🎸 Line A vs Line B — The Key Distinction
Line A ends deep and final (open D). Line B ends lighter and questioning (G fret 1). This alternation — resolved / unresolved — is what gives the mukhda its emotional push and pull. Once you feel it, the song makes musical sense.

⚠️ Sliding Technique
3/5 = play fret 3, slide finger up to fret 5 without lifting off. 5\3 = play fret 5, slide down to 3. The slide is one continuous motion — keep the string pressed the whole time or the note dies mid-slide.

Practice Plan
  1. Master Mukhda Line A first. Press ▶ above, listen, then mirror it. The 11-note G/B/D sequence is the DNA of the entire song.
  2. Feel Line A vs Line B. Only the final note differs — open D (resolved, deep) vs G fret 1 (questioning, lighter). Alternate between them until the difference feels natural.
  3. Practice the B-string slide in isolation. The pattern 3-3-3/5-3-0 needs a smooth continuous slide. Keep pressing throughout — don't lift off mid-slide.
  4. Learn the antara ascending run. B: 0-1-3-3-3-5-3-1-0 is a stepwise climb and descent. The B fret 5 is the emotional peak — give it a fraction more time.
  5. Recognise R.D. Burman's recycling. The bridge, "Tum mere ho" and "Hai vafa kya" all use the same sliding pattern. Female and male antaras share the same ascending run. Learn 4 patterns → you know the whole song.
  6. Play end to end. Use the structure map above as your roadmap. Use ▶ to verify each section before moving on.
